The process

Three steps to a result

Reading the guide takes hours. These three steps take two minutes.

1

Model your user inventory

Turn your team into role-based groups: headcount, the apps they need, and how deeply they use them, full users who run processes or light users who read and approve. The goal is to translate each role into a profile the licensing rules can understand.

2

Let the rules engine process it

The engine applies the rule set coded from the official guide: it picks the most expensive app as the base, converts the rest to attach licenses, checks Team Member eligibility against usage depth and custom app criteria, and enforces the Professional / Enterprise mixing ban across the whole environment.

3

Get an audited result

The output has three layers: a line-by-line cost breakdown per group, a gap analysis against the "full license for everyone" reference scenario, and a compliance report listing every rule that was applied and why. Monthly and yearly savings sit at the bottom.

How it works, a short guide

1. Split your users into groups. Everyone doing the same job is one group: "12 sales reps", "5 warehouse leads", "10 report viewers". For each group, enter the headcount and tick the apps they need (Sales, Customer Service, Field Service).

2. Pick a usage profile. If the group creates records and runs processes, choose "full user". If they mostly read data and approve things, choose "light user". Light users can often get by on the Team Member license, and that is where the biggest savings come from. For full users, tick the box if they need advanced features (forecasting, deep customization, omnichannel), because that decides between the Professional and Enterprise tiers.

The packages, the restrictions, and the logic behind them

What each license is, who it fits, and the rules to watch out for. Source: the Microsoft Dynamics 365 Licensing Guide.

Sales Professional

user / month
For teams with straightforward sales processes. The most economical full license when simple pipeline management is enough.
  • Covers: Leads, opportunities, quotes, basic reporting
  • Restriction: Can't be mixed with Enterprise in the same environment
  • Restriction: Limited customization (custom entity cap)

Sales Enterprise

user / month
The standard pick for teams with complex sales processes, forecasting needs and heavy customization.
  • Covers: Everything in Pro plus forecasting, Copilot, broad customization
  • Advantage: Acts as a qualifying base for attach licenses
  • Restriction: Can't be mixed with Professional in the same environment

Customer Service Pro / Enterprise

/ user / month
For customer service teams. Pro handles simple case management, Enterprise adds omnichannel and advanced scenarios.
  • Covers: Case management, knowledge base, self-service portal
  • Restriction: Pro and Enterprise can't be mixed in the same environment

Field Service

user / month
For field service teams: work orders, routing, technician management.
  • Covers: Work orders, scheduling, asset management, mobile
  • Advantage: Bought as a base, it lets other CE apps come in as attach

Team Member

user / month
For users who read data, approve records and make light updates. Used correctly, it's the single biggest savings lever.
  • Covers: Read access to all D365 data plus designated light scenarios
  • Restriction: No access to custom apps
  • Restriction: The Team Member app can hold at most 15 entities
  • Restriction: Technically enforced, no wiggle room

Attach licenses

user / month / app
The discounted model for a user's second and later apps. The most expensive app becomes the base, the rest attach.

In five minutes

Making sense of licensing logic

Four rules explain most of your invoice
01Base + attach is a discount on the second app

Microsoft licenses a user's first (most expensive) app at full price. The same user's second and later apps drop to a attach license per app. For someone doing both sales and customer service, base + attach instead of two full licenses adds up to a serious difference per user per month.

02Team Member is for light usage, not low usage

The Team Member license is designed for reading data, approving records and limited updates. Its limits are clear: at most 15 entities, no access to custom apps, and the restrictions are technically enforced. Assigned to the right user it's the biggest savings item, assigned to the wrong one it's an access incident.

03Tier choice is environment-wide, not per person

Professional and Enterprise licenses can't be mixed in the same environment. A single user's need for advanced features decides the tier for every full user of that app. This is the rule that most often slips through budget planning.

04The rules are getting real

Microsoft is tightening license assignment. On the Finance & Operations side, every user has needed an explicitly assigned license since November 2025, and non-compliant users are gradually losing access. Correct licensing is now as much about continuity as it is about savings.

The gray areas of licensing

This tool doesn't answer every question, and that's a deliberate choice. On the topics below, Microsoft's documentation is open to interpretation. The calculator produces no recommendations here and points you to your partner instead:

  • Multiplexing: Who needs a license when D365 data flows into other systems (a data warehouse, a portal, a reporting tool) through automated processes. The general rule is strict: anyone who sees the data needs a license, no matter how many systems sit in between. The edge cases are murky.
  • Dual-write / CRM-ERP integration: If a user's transaction is written to a second D365 system in the background, dual licensing may be required.
  • ISV solutions: Licensing requirements for third-party apps vary by vendor.

Dynamics 365 licensing is one of the least understood corners of the Microsoft ecosystem. Base and attach relationships, Team Member restrictions, the Professional and Enterprise mixing ban, all of it sits scattered across an official guide. The result: companies either pay for more than they need or run into access problems with non-compliant licenses.

This tool turns those rules into an open, free calculator anyone can use. It asks for no sign-up, collects no data and has no sales team behind it. The source code and pricing data are public on GitHub, and bug reports and contributions are open to everyone.

It was built by an independent developer and is not affiliated with Microsoft. Where things are unclear it doesn't calculate. It marks the gray areas of licensing openly and points you to your partner. A tool's credibility starts with being able to say what it doesn't know.
